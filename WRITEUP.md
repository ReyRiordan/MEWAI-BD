Our tool is a voice-to-voice AI simulation in which a medical student practices verbal de-escalation with an AI patient in real time. The student speaks freely (no scripted options, no forced turn-taking) and their words are transcribed, evaluated against a set of de-escalation actions, and used to drive a dynamically responding AI patient. An escalation bar (0–10) tracks patient agitation throughout the session. The goal is to reduce it to zero before the time limit expires. At an escalation level of 10 and beyond, the session immediately ends in failure.

A simulation case is defined with the initial context, goal, time limit, and a preset list of valid actions. Each action is defined with a corresponding change in escalation level and scene visuals. The overall scene is programmatically generated by combining transparent layers depending on the current state and active actions. Each turn of the simulation runs a pipeline: the student's speech is captured and transcribed, then the action detection AI agent evaluates the utterance against the scenario's defined action set, detecting any de-escalating or escalating behaviors from a the action list. Escalation updates and scene changes are applied immediately, and the patient AI agent — responding in character from the full conversation history and current agitation state — speaks its reply back through synthesized voice. The patient's tone, verbosity, and willingness to share clinical information all scale with escalation level.

During the session, feedback is ambient and non-interrupting: an escalation bar updates in real time (color-coded green to red), action badges flash briefly when a valid de-escalating or escalating action is detected, scene images change dynamically to reflect environmental state changes, and a live transcript logs the exchange. At session end, a debrief screen displays the complete action checklist — every scenario action marked as correct or incorrect/missed — alongside outcome, time taken to de-escalate, escalation bar trajectory over time, and final escalation score. This enables objective competence evaluation and provides the student with immediate, targeted feedback.

The core design decision was modality: verbal de-escalation is a spoken skill, and practicing it by clicking through menus or typing responses would be a categorically different cognitive and motor task. Our tool is voice-to-voice because that is what real patient de-escalation skill requires. It can also be distinguished from common existing alternatives in a few concrete ways. Standardized patient (SP)-based training requires scheduling, trained actors, and institutional coordination — it cannot scale to on-demand, repeated practice. Virtual reality approaches require specialized hardware and rely on pre-scripted branching logic, meaning patient behavior follows a decision tree rather than responding genuinely to what the learner says. Our AI patient is not pre-scripted: it responds dynamically to the full conversation context, so no two sessions unfold identically, and a student cannot easily memorize a winning script. The tool runs in a browser, requires no hardware beyond a microphone, and can be used at any time, supporting both independent self-directed practice and faculty-assigned curricular exercises.

To conclude, our tool presents a novel de-escalation simulation framework that is both realistic and scalable, seeking to address many of the challenges and limitations faced by past training methodologies.
